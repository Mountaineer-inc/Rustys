Section 03

The Strategic Reframe

We're not rebuilding a wholesale manufacturing operation. We're building a DTC brand that happens to manufacture its own product.

The thesis: DTC as the primary channel (60–70% of revenue, 75–85% margins). Wholesale rebuilt in parallel, but never load-bearing again. One account collapsing shouldn't collapse the business.

Why this works

  • Proven product with real retail track record, not a hypothetical
  • Formulations are documented and in-hand
  • Bass Pro door stays open on our timeline, not theirs
  • DTC creates consumer demand independent of any wholesale buyer's mood
  • Joseph's brand background from the outdoor industry de-risks the DTC build

Unit economics support it

COGS Range
$1.11–$2.27

Per unit across top 3 SKUs. Shad bag $1.23, Chicken Livers $1.11, Sponge Bait $2.27.

Retail Range
$5.99–$9.99

4oz bags $5.99–6.99, 12oz Sponge Bait $7.99–9.99.

DTC Margin
75–85%

On DTC channel. Wholesale margins lower but volume-supported.

Competitive retail context: Triple S $30/45oz ($0.67/oz), Team Catfish Secret 7 ~$30, Magic Bait $6–9, Mr. Whiskers $8.95/12oz. Middle-premium positioning is credible and defensible.

Section 05

Facility and Operations

Zone layout (Month 1)

The owned building gets set up to handle current volume plus 3x projected, no more. Expansion is triggered by sustained demand, not preemptive.

  • Production — Mixing, formaldehyde storage with compliant ventilation, equipment positioned for expansion. Capacity 2,000–8,000 units/month.
  • Packaging/QC — Packaging line with room for a second station, QC, labeling, boxing.
  • Fulfillment — Packing, shipping supplies staging, small parcel + pallet area. Capacity 500–1,000 orders/month.
  • Inventory — Finished goods, raw materials, packaging supplies. Capacity $80–120K value.

Facility setup cost

  • Equipment move: $8–10K
  • Shelving, utilities, signage: $4–6K
  • Total: $12–16K (separate from the $130K envelope)
Note

Regulatory compliance setup is additional. Expect $14–34K in required one-time compliance costs (base case); up to $43K with optional additions. Building code occupancy confirmed F-1 — no Group H contingency. See the Regulatory document for full picture.

Equipment activation plan

Use immediately

1 weigher, 1 vacuum sealer (the better of VP540/VP545), 1 air compressor, mixers, grinders, heat gun, scales.

Assess for repair

7 non-working vacuum sealers. If repair is under $200/unit, fix 2–3 as backups. Three vacuum pumps — same call.

Stage for scale

2nd weigher (activate at 4,000+ units/month). 2nd vacuum sealer as backup.

Do not activate yet

Forklifts. Not needed until daily pallet moves — likely Year 2+.

Production restart (Month 2–3)

Recipes are in-hand. The work is verifying each recipe against seller's video walkthrough, producing small test batches (100–200 units per top-3 SKU, then 300–500), testing packaging and QC, and fulfilling existing wholesale orders while building process confidence before scaling.

SOPs to write Month 2–3

Formaldehyde safety protocols. Equipment setup and operation. Batch production start-to-finish. Packaging process. QC checklist. Equipment cleaning and maintenance. Inventory FIFO rotation. Waste disposal (formaldehyde-containing). Written as Google Docs with photos and video, migrated to a formal system if scale requires.

Critical Path

Figure 03
Milestone Timeline — Month 0 through Month 12
Full DTC launch (M6) is the primary inflection point. Joseph runs production hands-on through Month 8 (target), then transitions to strategic oversight as the Operations Manager onboards Month 9–10.

Pre-close (Weeks −2 to 0)

Finalize agreement, plan equipment move, begin regulatory prep — zoning confirmation, permit queue, compliance calls scheduled for Week 1.

Month 1

  • Week 1–2 — Close, secure assets, seller handoff (recipes + videos), move equipment, start regulatory compliance calls
  • Week 3–4 — Set up production zones, supplier verification calls (Ben E Keith, JD Cooley, Brenntag first), Joseph begins hands-on production

Month 2

  • Week 5–6 — IMS implementation, Joseph hands-on production, regulatory calls complete (zoning, NCDA&CS, Fire Marshal), packaging inventory audit and ordering
  • Week 7–8 — Retail outreach begins, brand identity work starts, Pro-Staff Manager conversations, SOP documentation underway

Month 3

  • Week 9–10 — Joseph hands-on production, SOPs in full working draft, test batches across core SKUs
  • Week 11–12 — Formulation verification complete, production process documented, website build begins, product photography

Months 4–5

Inventory and packaging procurement (8–12 week lead times make this a Month 3–4 priority). Website development, packaging design, content creation, launch photography. Joseph still hands-on in production while brand work runs in parallel.

Month 5

Soft launch to friends, family, beta testers. 50–100 test orders. Fix what breaks.

Month 6

Full DTC launch. Paid ads live, PR push, email marketing begins, Pro-Staff publicly announced. Begin Operations Manager search (open market, Months 6–7).

Months 5–8 — Transition Target

Joseph begins pulling back from daily production in areas where the process is stable and documented. This is a target, not a guarantee — pace is set by how quickly SOPs hold without Joseph's direct oversight. Full brand, marketing, and strategic work runs alongside production in this window. Part-time contractor fills production hours as Joseph steps back.

Months 9–10 — Operations Manager Onboard

Operations Manager starts. Shadow period: runs production alongside Joseph for full process transfer. Joseph available for questions and QC sign-off. OM takes increasing ownership of daily production through Month 10.

Month 11 — Joseph Out of Production

Joseph fully strategic. Brand, marketing, wholesale relationships, financial oversight. Operations Manager owns daily production, QC, and fulfillment. Year 2 planning begins: DTC performance review, wholesale pipeline, Production Associate #1 evaluation if volume warrants.

Section 12

Risks

Risk 01 — Supplier Discontinuation or Price Increase

Backups identified, tiered pricing negotiated, relationships built. Fallback: reformulate.

Risk 02 — DTC CAC Higher Than Projected

Small paid tests first, optimize before scaling, lean on organic and Pro-Staff if paid underperforms. Never scale a losing channel.

Risk 03 — Production Quality Issues

Rigorous QC, test batches, documented processes. Fallback: immediate recall, replace, overcommunicate. QC is non-negotiable.

Risk 04 — Formaldehyde Regulatory Compliance

Schedule NC DOL free consultation (OSH Consultative Services, 919-807-2899) — replaces most paid EHS consulting. Contracted items: industrial hygienist for air monitoring ($1,500–3,000) and occupational health provider for medical surveillance. Ventilation and PPE in place, training documented. See Regulatory document for full picture. Fallback: reformulate without formaldehyde (Year 2 product development consideration).

Risk 05 — Folsom Continues Declining

DTC-first is the hedge. Not load-bearing on any single account. If Folsom goes to zero, the model still works.

Risk 06 — Bass Pro Never Reactivates

Already planned around. Grow Scheels, Big Rock, and new regional chains. Bass Pro is upside, not the plan.

Risk 07 — Joseph Bandwidth Across Construction Brands

Hire strong Lead early, build systems that don't require Joseph daily. Fallback: additional contractor support or earlier Ops Manager promotion.

Risk 08 — Seasonality Worse Than Expected

Build reserves in peak, plan for 3–4 slow months, line of credit as backup. Subscription SKUs partially hedge seasonality.

Risk 09 — Equipment Failure

Maintenance protocol established. Backup vacuum sealer ready. Repair non-working units as additional backups before they're needed.
