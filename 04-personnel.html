Prepared for Joseph Skinner
Updated April 2026
Team Size Y1 4 roles
01

Operating Principle

The previous ownership stalled at $52K because one person can only do so much. We are not making that mistake. But we are also not making the opposite mistake — hiring a full team before revenue supports it and watching Year 1 bleed out.

Hire for the work that exists, with a clear trigger for the work that's coming.

Every role has a reason it exists now and a performance trigger for when it scales. No "nice to have" roles in Year 1.

The Math We're Protecting

Joseph's comp comes from Don's payroll across construction businesses — not from Rusty's P&L. That keeps Year 1 labor lean: Joseph runs production hands-on through Month 8 (target). A part-time contractor supports production throughout. The Operations Manager comes on board Month 9–10 when process is stable and documented. Pro-Staff Manager (fractional) and Easton round out the year. Total labor cost to Rusty's P&L: ~$67–93K.

Why Month 9–10 and Not Month 3

The original plan had this hire in Month 3. That was wrong. Handing production to someone new in Month 3 means handing them a process that isn't fully documented, that Joseph hasn't deeply learned, and that no one has run at real volume yet. The right sequence is: Joseph builds the process and documents it (Months 1–8), then hands it to someone qualified enough to own it outright. That person starts in Month 9–10 with a real SOP library and a working production process, not a pile of unknowns.

◆

What the Role Owns

  • Daily production — mixing, formulating, batching, QC
  • Packaging and fulfillment — orders out the door
  • Inventory management and reorder triggers
  • Equipment operation and maintenance
  • Compliance oversight — formaldehyde handling, NC food manufacturing standards
  • Safety and SOP execution
  • Supervision of Easton during production hours (Year 1)
  • Supervision of Production Associates as team scales (Year 2+)

This Is Not an Entry-Level Role

The comp reflects the scope. Someone at $75–85K annualized is expected to own the operations function with minimal hand-holding — not to be trained on what "production management" means. They take the SOPs Joseph wrote and run them. They identify where the SOPs are wrong and fix them. They manage Easton, later manage associates. They are the person who makes the building work when Joseph isn't in it.

Paying $50–60K gets a production worker who needs a manager. Paying $75–85K gets the manager. At Year 1 volumes, we need the manager — not another set of hands.

Compensation Structure

Phase Base Notes
Year 1 (Months 9–12) $75–85K ann. ~$29–36K cost to Y1 P&L at 3.5 months
Year 2 $75–85K + bonus Volume and quality bonuses per working draft below
Year 3 / Exit $80–95K + transaction bonus If business exits: 1–2% of sale price as transaction bonus. Retains through closing.

Bonus Structure (Working Draft)

  • Volume: Bonus when monthly unit output hits a consistent threshold
  • Quality: Tied to customer complaint rate and QC metrics
  • Growth: When role expands to managing a team of 2–4 associates, base moves to $80–95K
  • Transaction bonus: 1–2% of sale price upon business exit. Reflects the contribution an operations owner makes to business value and keeps the right person in place through closing.

Candidate Profile

  • Food manufacturing or industrial production background — 3–7 years
  • Has managed a production function before, not just worked in one
  • Detail-oriented, safety-conscious, comfortable with compliance documentation
  • Comfortable with physical work and a variable task list
  • Growth-oriented — sees this as a functional ownership role, not a job
  • Stable enough in life to stick around 2–3 years through an exit event

Search Strategy

Open market. Search begins Month 6–7 to allow adequate lead time for Month 9–10 start. This role is not built around any individual currently in the picture — the spec is written for the best available candidate.

Year 2–3 Expansion

Year 2: OM takes full ownership of production, fulfillment, and Easton replacement hires. Likely managing 1 Production Associate. Base moves to $80–95K range at that team size.

Year 3: Operations running independently of Joseph. OM is effectively the COO-equivalent for an exit-ready business. Transaction bonus terms (1–2% of sale price) create alignment between OM incentives and ownership's exit goals.

Candidate Engagement Status

A specific candidate from Joseph’s network has confirmed interest in principle. His condition: product quality needs to be real before he commits publicly to the brand. What Easton Actually Does

Months 5–8 — Expanded Scope

As DTC volume ramps and Joseph begins transitioning out of daily production, Easton's role expands in kind. More fulfillment volume means more time on packing and shipping. More inventory throughput means more FIFO rotation, receiving, and counting. More admin as the business gets busier means more order processing and data entry. All of this stays within Zone A — no new regulated tasks, no expansion into production responsibility. The scope grows, the legal framework stays the same.

Production and Fulfillment (Primary)

  • Batch production assistance under Lead/OM supervision
  • Packaging and labeling
  • Order fulfillment — pack, label, ship
  • Inventory counting and receiving
  • Facility organization and cleanup

Content Capture (Secondary — High Value)

  • He loves to fish and is an avid outdoorsman. This is gold.
  • Fishing trips with Rusty's product become authentic content — not forced, not promotional
  • Photo and video capture at the water — catches, rigging, on-the-bank usage
  • Social media content contribution with oversight for brand voice
  • Field testing new formulations and providing feedback as a real target-demo user
Why This Works

Paying market rate changes the relationship from "owner's kid hanging around" to "employee with accountability and a development path." He has to show up on time, do the work, take feedback like any other employee. That's the real lesson in how a business actually runs.

A 16-year-old avid outdoorsman posting real catch content with Rusty's is exactly the kind of authentic audience-facing material the brand strategy calls for. Zero creator acquisition cost. Real audience perspective in product feedback.

Legal Employment Framework — Zone A Only

Easton's scope is governed by NC Wage and Hour Act (NCGS § 95-25.5), FLSA Hazardous Orders, the NC Detrimental Occupations list, and OSHA 29 CFR 1910.1048 (the formaldehyde standard). Because formaldehyde is present in the facility, his employment is more restricted than a typical 16-year-old retail or warehouse job. The framework is clear — it just requires documentation and zone discipline.

Zone A — What He Does
  • Office, admin, order processing
  • Packaging and labeling (sealed goods)
  • Inventory, FIFO rotation (dry goods)
  • Content capture — photo/video
  • Social media and marketing support
  • Product field testing (fishing trips)
  • Facility org and cleanup (non-production areas)
Zone B — Firm Prohibitions
  • Formaldehyde handling — no exceptions
  • Active production zone at ≥0.5 ppm
  • Forklift / Komatsu FG25C operation
  • Any respirator-required tasks
  • Confined space entry
  • Batch production responsibility

A Youth Employment Certificate must be obtained from NC DOL (1-800-625-2267) before his first day — required by NCGS § 95-25.5. A written job description documenting his scope and restrictions must be signed before work begins. Operations Manager is responsible for zone enforcement — this is not Easton's job to manage.

Full Framework

Complete legal citations, zone definitions, documentation checklist, and prohibited tasks with citations: see Regulatory Doc — Section 04: Youth Employment.

What Easton Does Not Do

  • Formaldehyde handling — OSHA 29 CFR 1910.1048 / NC Detrimental Occupations, no exceptions
  • Forklift or powered industrial vehicle operation — FLSA Hazardous Order #7, no exceptions under 18
  • Any task requiring a respirator — NC Detrimental Occupation #9
  • Active production zone when formaldehyde operations are running — OSHA 29 CFR 1910.1048 Action Level (0.5 ppm)
  • Supplier negotiations or contract decisions
  • Any money decisions — purchasing, AR, payroll
  • Difficult customer situations or complaint handling
  • Hiring involvement — can observe, not participate

Trade-Off Awareness

  • Time. If 8–10 of Joseph's 20–30 hours go to design and website work in Months 3–5, something else gets shorted — likely strategic brand work, pro-staff program development, or retail relationships.
  • Quality ceiling. A specialist designer or photographer might produce work 20–30% better in ways customers perceive. The question is whether that incremental quality drives enough revenue to justify $15–25K in Year 1 contract costs. Probably not at this scale.
  • When to hand it off. Year 2–3, when the business can sustain $30–40K/year in contract creative work without hurting P&L, execution moves to contractors or a hire. The marker is revenue, not preference.
The Upside

Joseph owning creative through launch means the brand voice is consistent, tightly held, and not filtered through third parties who don't know the audience. The $15–25K in savings either goes to profit (better exit position), gets redeployed to the pro-staff program or paid media, or offsets the higher Operations Manager salary.

Hiring Principles

Things to remember every time we're about to add someone.

1. Hire for the work that exists, not the work you hope will exist.

Trigger-based hiring. Volume must come before the hire, not after.

2. The first hire in a function defines the function.

Operations Manager's quality determines whether we can scale to 500+ orders or stall at 100. Pro-Staff Manager's quality determines whether the creator program becomes the growth engine or an expensive list of dead accounts. Hire slow, fire fast.

3. Pay for the work, not the title.

$50–60K Operations Manager is expensive relative to $40K, but cheap relative to rehiring in Month 8 because $40K got us someone who can't grow with us.

4. Contracts over full-time until proven.

Principle

Pro-Staff Manager stays fractional until the volume forces the conversation. Same for any future specialist roles — content, community, ecommerce. Prove the need before the commitment.

5. Don't solve short-term problems with long-term hires.

Temporary production help stays temporary. One-off project work stays contractor. A busy month is not a hiring signal.

6. The Rusty's team is small on purpose.

Lean is the strategy. A 6-person team running a $325–400K business with $150–220K SDE is what makes the exit attractive. Bloat kills the multiple.
