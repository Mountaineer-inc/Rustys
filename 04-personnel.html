Personnel & Team

Who does what, how much time, how much cost, when

Prepared for Joseph Skinner
Updated April 2026
Team Size Y1 4 roles
01

Operating Principle

The previous ownership stalled at $52K because one person can only do so much. We are not making that mistake. But we are also not making the opposite mistake — hiring a full team before revenue supports it and watching Year 1 bleed out.

Hire for the work that exists, with a clear trigger for the work that's coming.

Every role has a reason it exists now and a performance trigger for when it scales. No "nice to have" roles in Year 1.

The Math We're Protecting

Joseph's comp comes from Don's payroll across construction businesses — not from Rusty's P&L. That keeps Year 1 labor budget focused on the three people actually needed to run the business: Operations Manager, Pro-Staff Manager (fractional), and Easton (part-time). Plus a contract roster for one-off project work.

Why $50–60K and Not $40–50K

The original doc had this role at $40–50K, but the scope demands more. This isn't a production worker with a narrow job description — it's the person who owns everything that happens inside the building. Production, fulfillment, compliance, inventory, equipment, safety, Easton's daily supervision. That's a management role from Day 1, and the comp needs to reflect it.

  • Daily production — mixing, formulating, batching, QC
  • Packaging and fulfillment — orders out the door
  • Inventory management and reorder triggers
  • Equipment operation and maintenance
  • Compliance oversight — formaldehyde handling, NC food manufacturing standards
  • Safety and SOPs
  • Supervision of Easton during production hours
  • Supervision of Production Associate when hired (Month 8–10 trigger)
  • Expanded scope to managing 2–4 associates at Month 16–20 revenue trigger, with comp moving to $65–75K
The Comp Logic

Paying $40K gets a production worker. Paying $50–60K gets someone who can own the function from the start and grow into managing a team. The incremental $10–20K is cheap compared to rehiring in Month 8 because the first hire couldn't grow with us.

Profile

  • Food manufacturing or industrial production background — 2–5 years
  • Detail-oriented, safety-conscious
  • Comfortable with physical work and a variable task list
  • Willing to be hands-on now and manage later
  • Growth-oriented
  • Stable enough in life to stick around 2–3 years — this isn't a stepping-stone role

Bonus Structure (Working Draft)

  • Volume: Bonus when monthly unit output hits threshold consistently
  • Quality: Tied to customer complaint rate and QC metrics
  • Growth: When role expands to managing a team of 2–4 associates, base moves to $65–75K

Content Capture (Secondary — High Value)

  • Photo and video capture at the water — catches, rigging, on-the-bank usage

Why This Works

Paying market rate changes the relationship from "owner's kid hanging around" to "employee with accountability and a development path." He has to show up on time, do the work, take feedback like any other employee. That's the real lesson in how a business actually runs.

A 16-year-old avid outdoorsman posting real catch content with Rusty's is exactly the kind of authentic audience-facing material the brand strategy calls for. Zero creator acquisition cost. Real audience perspective in product feedback.

What Easton Does Not Do

  • Formaldehyde handling — liability, age restrictions, no exceptions
  • Supplier negotiations or contract decisions
  • Any money decisions — purchasing, AR, payroll
  • Difficult customer situations or complaint handling
  • Hiring involvement — can observe, not participate

College Consideration

Easton heads to college in roughly 2 years. The plan accounts for him being far less available starting late Year 2. He is not the hire we build around. He is a genuinely useful set of hands now and a development investment for Don.

Creative & Content Work

Joseph is handling the majority of design, photography, development, and copywriting in-house during Year 1. This is a meaningful cost avoidance — roughly $25–35K of contract work absorbed into his existing time commitment.

What Joseph Owns In-House

  • Brand identity direction and execution — logo development, visual system, brand guidelines. May still engage a contract illustrator for specific assets if the work exceeds his tools.
  • Website build — Shopify store configuration, theme customization, product pages, basic store setup.
  • Copywriting — site copy, product descriptions, email flows, blog posts, ad copy.
  • Photography — product and lifestyle using existing equipment or iPhone 15 Pro. Product shots, behind-the-scenes, in-use content with Easton fishing.
  • Video (basic) — short-form content for social, simple product videos, ad creative.

What Still Needs Contract Support

Role When Cost Purpose
Pro photographer Month 4–5 $1–2K Hero product photography for website and packaging. One-time professional shoot for lasting brand assets.
Videographer Month 5–6 $1–2K One or two key launch videos. After launch, Joseph + pro-staff handle ongoing video.
PR consultant Month 6–8 $2–4K Launch PR push. Joseph can write pitches but doesn't have the media relationships a consultant brings.
Specialist designer Year 1 ad hoc $2–3K Packaging illustration, custom mark work, anything outside Joseph's tool set.
Total contract roster Year 1 ~$6–11K Down from original $26–40K estimate.

Trade-Off Awareness

  • Time. If 8–10 of Joseph's 20–30 hours go to design and website work in Months 3–5, something else gets shorted — likely strategic brand work, pro-staff program development, or retail relationships.
  • Quality ceiling. A specialist designer or photographer might produce work 20–30% better in ways customers perceive. The question is whether that incremental quality drives enough revenue to justify $15–25K in Year 1 contract costs. Probably not at this scale.
  • When to hand it off. Year 2–3, when the business can sustain $30–40K/year in contract creative work without hurting P&L, execution moves to contractors or a hire. The marker is revenue, not preference.
The Upside

Joseph owning creative through launch means the brand voice is consistent, tightly held, and not filtered through third parties who don't know the audience. The $15–25K in savings either goes to profit (better exit position), gets redeployed to the pro-staff program or paid media, or offsets the higher Operations Manager salary.

Don's ask is that Easton learns how to run a business by working inside one. That only happens if there's a structure, not just tasks.

Weekly Cadence

  • Production and fulfillment work — primary job
  • Wednesday 30-minute 1-on-1 with Joseph — not a task check-in. A business education check-in: what did you observe, what questions came up, what would you do differently.

  • Joseph–Easton–Don sit-down (30 min) to review progress and adjust
◆

Year 1 Goal

Easton finishes Year 1 understanding the full business cycle: how product is made, how it's sold, how customers are acquired, how the money works, how decisions get made. Not expert in any of it. Literate in all of it.

Year 2 Goal

Easton starts contributing to decisions, not just executing on them. Proposes a marketing initiative. Runs a small project — a tournament booth activation or a content campaign. Makes a vendor call under supervision. Begins to develop professional judgment, not just work output.

What Easton Is Not

Not the heir apparent getting groomed for succession. Not a special employee who gets away with less. Not a shadow. He's a part-time employee who happens to be the owner's son, getting genuine business education as a byproduct of doing real work with real accountability. If he shows interest and capability over 2–3 years, Don makes decisions about his future from an informed position. If he's more interested in something else by then, that's fine too — he leaves with a foundation that's useful regardless.

Hiring Principles

Things to remember every time we're about to add someone.

1. Hire for the work that exists, not the work you hope will exist.

Trigger-based hiring. Volume must come before the hire, not after.

2. The first hire in a function defines the function.

Operations Manager's quality determines whether we can scale to 500+ orders or stall at 100. Pro-Staff Manager's quality determines whether the creator program becomes the growth engine or an expensive list of dead accounts. Hire slow, fire fast.

3. Pay for the work, not the title.

$50–60K Operations Manager is expensive relative to $40K, but cheap relative to rehiring in Month 8 because $40K got us someone who can't grow with us.

4. Contracts over full-time until proven.

Principle

Pro-Staff Manager stays fractional until the volume forces the conversation. Same for any future specialist roles — content, community, ecommerce. Prove the need before the commitment.

5. Don't solve short-term problems with long-term hires.

Temporary production help stays temporary. One-off project work stays contractor. A busy month is not a hiring signal.

6. The Rusty's team is small on purpose.

Lean is the strategy. A 6-person team running a $325–400K business with $150–220K SDE is what makes the exit attractive. Bloat kills the multiple.
