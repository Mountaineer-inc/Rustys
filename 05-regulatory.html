Section 01

Zoning and Land Use — The Gating Question

Gating Item — Highest Month 1 Priority

This is the single most important compliance question before production restart. The Month 1 call to Buncombe County Planning (828-250-4830) takes priority over all other regulatory steps.

The Finding

The current property is zoned CS (Commercial Service) / Multi-Use Commercial. Per Buncombe County Code of Ordinances, Chapter 78, Section 78-641, Table 1 — Permitted Use Table, manufacturing and processing operations are not a permitted use in the CS district.

The zoning districts that permit manufacturing are: EMP (Employment District), AI (Airport Industry District), and OU (Open Use District).

Per Section 78-640(f), the CS district purpose is "clustered commercial development… commercial activity in those specified areas with access to major traffic arteries." It is designed for retail, offices, restaurants, and service-oriented commercial uses — not production operations.

This is not a gray area. The ordinance is explicit: manufacturing requires EMP, AI, or OU zoning. CS does not have it.

Three Paths — In Detail

Path 1 — Preferred
Grandfathered Nonconforming Use

If prior manufacturing was active at this address before the current zoning took effect, the property may carry grandfathered nonconforming use status. This is common with older buildings in corridors later rezoned commercial.

Four key questions to resolve with Planning:

  • Does this parcel have nonconforming use status on record for manufacturing or industrial activity?
  • Does nonconforming status transfer with acquisition, or does it attach only to the prior operator?
  • What is the abandonment rule? Nonconforming uses typically lapse if discontinued for 6–12 months. If the prior owner ceased manufacturing before the sale closed, the status may already be lost.
  • Would any expansion or modification of the manufacturing use trigger loss of nonconforming status?

If grandfathered: get the determination in writing. Keep it in the permanent compliance file.

Path 2 — Formal Process
Rezoning to EMP or OU

Formal process through the Planning Board and County Commissioners. Takes months, not days. Not guaranteed. Adjacent zoning patterns and neighbor support matter. Public hearing required.

This is the right path only if nonconforming status does not exist and the location is fixed. Do not begin this process before the Month 1 Planning call confirms that Path 1 is closed.

Path 3 — Closed
Alternative Paths (Variance / CUP)

Variance is unlikely because variances address hardship, not use changes. Conditional use permit appears closed — the ordinance doesn't list manufacturing as a conditional use in CS (no "C" in Table 1 for that use category).

These paths exist in principle but are not realistic here. Do not invest time pursuing them before confirming Path 1 and Path 2 are exhausted.

The Month 1 Action

"What is the current zoning of [address], and does the property have any nonconforming use status on record for manufacturing or industrial activity?" Buncombe County Planning & Development — (828) 250-4830
Call — Free — 15 Minutes — Month 1 Priority

If yes (nonconforming confirmed): get it in writing. Confirm transfer rules and abandonment period. If no: the conversation about rezoning versus an alternative location happens before any further compliance or equipment spending.

The Stakes

If the property does not have grandfathered nonconforming manufacturing status, the business cannot legally operate as a manufacturer at that address under current zoning. The "owned building" advantage that saves $18–24K/year on rent — a material driver of Year 1 P&L — may not be usable for manufacturing without rezoning or a formal nonconforming designation.

This is why Don is actively searching for a permanent, properly zoned and classified facility. The current building is being treated as interim for this and other reasons. See Section A — Interim Operating Strategy for the interim operating posture.

OSHA Formaldehyde Standard (29 CFR 1910.1048) — Confirmed Applies

This is the most operationally consequential regulation we face. Sourced directly from the eCFR and OSHA enforcement materials.

Enforcement context: A NC cabinet manufacturer was fined $47,000 after air sampling showed 1.2 ppm average. Remediation cost $180,000 in ventilation and reformulation. This is the class of operation OSHA actively inspects.

Initial Monitoring

Every employer with formaldehyde exposure must conduct initial exposure monitoring within first operations. Conducted by an industrial hygienist with passive diffusion badges or active sampling equipment and accredited lab analysis. Required within 30 days of production start. Rusty's formaldehyde solutions exceed the 0.1% exception threshold — we need to monitor.

Periodic Monitoring

  • Every 6 months if exposure is at or above Action Level (0.5 ppm)
  • Annually if above STEL (2 ppm)
  • Can reduce frequency after two consecutive samples below Action Level

Hierarchy of Controls (Required by OSHA)

  1. Substitution with less hazardous materials — the goal of Document 06
  2. Engineering controls (ventilation, enclosed processes) — primary expected control at Rusty's
  3. Administrative controls — work scheduling, reduced exposure duration
  4. PPE (respirators) — last resort, never a substitute for engineering controls

Engineering Controls Specifically Required

  • Local exhaust ventilation at emission points (mixing stations, formaldehyde addition)
  • General dilution ventilation to reduce ambient concentration
  • Enclosed processes where feasible
  • Air changes per hour sufficient to maintain below Action Level

Respiratory Protection Program — 29 CFR 1910.134

Triggered if engineering and work practice controls alone cannot reduce exposure below the PEL; during installation or repair of engineering controls; in emergency situations; or when an employee voluntarily requests protection. Written program required. Fit-testing by contractor ($75–150/person).

Medical Surveillance

Required if employee is exposed at or above Action Level (0.5 ppm): baseline exam before exposure, annual exams thereafter, examination within 24 hours of any emergency exposure, medical removal protection if physician determines health impairment. Contracted occupational health provider — approximately $150–300 per employee per year.

Training — At Least Annually

Required for all employees exposed at or above 0.1 ppm. Free OSHA training materials available at osha.gov — this is a DIY task. Content required:

  • Health effects of formaldehyde — including carcinogenicity
  • Engineering and administrative controls in place
  • Safe work practices and PPE use
  • Emergency procedures, spill response
  • Signs and symptoms of overexposure
  • Rights under the standard — access to medical records and monitoring results

Regulated Areas — Required Signage

Where exposure may exceed the PEL or STEL, post signs bearing exactly:

DANGER — FORMALDEHYDE — MAY CAUSE CANCER — CAUSES SKIN, EYE, AND RESPIRATORY IRRITATION — AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL ONLY

Recordkeeping

  • Exposure records: 30 years
  • Medical records: duration of employment + 30 years
  • Respirator fit testing records: until replaced by new test
  • Training records: duration of employment

Hazard Communication — 29 CFR 1910.1200

  • SDS (Safety Data Sheets) for every formaldehyde product — accessible to workers at all times in a binder on the production floor
  • Labels per GHS standards on all containers
  • Chemical inventory list maintained and updated

Emergency Procedures

  • Spill response plan with appropriate neutralizer — sodium bisulfite is the primary recommended neutralizer
  • Eyewash stations and safety showers within 10 seconds' travel time per ANSI Z358.1
  • Written evacuation procedures, posted floor plan, designated assembly point

What This Looks Like for Rusty's

  • Dedicated formaldehyde storage and mixing area with source-capture exhaust ventilation
  • Nitrile gloves, splash goggles, chemical-resistant apron at all mixing stations
  • Written OSHA Formaldehyde Protection Program DIY — 29 CFR 1910.1048 is the template
  • Initial air monitoring within 30 days of production start — contracted industrial hygienist, ~$1,500–3,000
  • SDS binder accessible to all production staff, updated when products change DIY
  • Warning signage posted in all regulated areas DIY
  • Eyewash/shower station installed per ANSI Z358.1 — non-negotiable
  • Annual medical surveillance for Operations Manager and regularly exposed associates
  • Formaldehyde spill kit with sodium bisulfite on production floor at all times
  • Annual training documented with signatures and date DIY

Training — Who This Applies To

Employer-of-Record Is Irrelevant to OSHA Obligations

OSHA training and exposure protection requirements attach to the work being performed, not to how the worker is paid or which entity cuts their check. A contractor billed through a separate payroll company, a W2 employee of a related entity, and a direct Rusty's W2 hire are all subject to the same OSHA 29 CFR 1910.1048 training and medical surveillance obligations if they have potential formaldehyde exposure. There is no payroll arrangement that exempts a person from this obligation.

EPA RCRA Hazardous Waste — Confirmed Applies

Sourced from 40 CFR 261.33, EPA formaldehyde guidance, and NCDEQ Hazardous Waste Section.

Formaldehyde Is Federally Classified Hazardous Waste

  • 40 CFR 261.33 classifies discarded commercial chemical products containing formaldehyde as hazardous waste — EPA code U122
  • 40 CFR 116.4 — formaldehyde is a Clean Water Act designated hazardous substance
  • Clean Air Act Section 112 — formaldehyde is a Hazardous Air Pollutant (HAP)

NC DEQ Division of Waste Management Hazardous Waste Section administers federal RCRA regulations in North Carolina. NC has state RCRA authority and has adopted federal generator categories and reporting requirements.

Section 04

NC Building Code — F-1 Classification (Locked)

Classification: Confirmed and Locked

Factory Industrial F-1 (Moderate Hazard). This determination is locked. F-1 is the outcome. Group H contingency has been removed from the analysis. The operation is designed to stay clearly below Maximum Allowable Quantity thresholds.

F-1 covers light manufacturing including food processing and chemical blending at quantities below high-hazard thresholds. Formaldehyde quantities are managed below the MAQ thresholds set out in Tables 307.1(1) and 307.1(2) of the NC Building Code (based on IBC Chapter 3). MAQ without sprinklers for toxic liquids: approximately 500 lbs. With sprinklers and approved storage: that threshold doubles. The operation is designed to stay comfortably under MAQ.

What F-1 Requires

  • Standard commercial construction
  • Control area design to manage hazardous material quantities
  • Normal fire-rated separations per Chapter 6
  • Standard sprinkler system (not the enhanced Group H suppression system)
  • Code-compliant egress per Chapter 10

Why F-1 Classification Matters Financially

Group H (High Hazard) occupancy would add $30,000–80,000 to build-out cost through enhanced suppression systems, explosion-proof electrical, more restrictive construction assemblies, and additional permitting. Staying F-1 by managing formaldehyde quantities below MAQ thresholds is the difference between a manageable compliance build-out and a facility-changing capital expense. This is not assumed — it is designed in, and it is locked.

What's Required Regardless

A licensed NC mechanical engineer to design the ventilation system is required before construction. One-time engagement: $1,500–4,000. This is an engineering design confirmation and ventilation specification — not an occupancy classification analysis. The classification question is resolved. The engineering engagement is about designing a compliant ventilation system that keeps air monitoring results below Action Level.

Change of Occupancy

NC Existing Building Code Chapter 10 may require a Change of Occupancy review if the building's use changes from its last certified occupancy. Confirm the building's currently-certified occupancy during the Month 1 Planning call. If triggered: permits, $250–1,000 and a few weeks. Not a deal-breaker; just a step. Contact Buncombe County Permits & Inspections: (828) 250-5360.

Youth Employment — Easton's Scope of Work

Easton is 16, the owner's son, and a part-time team member. NC and federal law impose restrictions on what minors under 18 can do in a manufacturing environment. The formaldehyde piece makes this more constrained than a typical teen job.

The Rules

NC Wage and Hour Act (NCGS § 95-25.5) governs minors 14–17 in NC. A Youth Employment Certificate is required before work begins. Hour restrictions and a prohibited occupations list apply.

Work hour rules for 16–17 year olds in NC:

  • No daily or weekly hour limits for 16–17 (unlike 14–15 year olds, who have stricter caps)
  • Cannot work between 11:00 PM and 5:00 AM on school nights if enrolled in grade 12 or lower — NCGS § 95-25.5(c)
  • Work permit (Youth Employment Certificate) is required before starting — from the school administration, not NC DOL directly

The hazardous and detrimental occupations list applies until he turns 18. Federal FLSA Hazardous Orders (HO 1–17) and NC's Detrimental Occupations List both impose specific restrictions beyond general hour rules.

Section 06

What Does Not Apply — Verified Against Primary Sources

Three regulatory categories were considered and ruled out based on primary-source research. These are documented here so they don't get revisited.

1 — NC Commercial Feed Law — Does Not Apply

Statute: NCGS § 106-284.33(4) — NC Commercial Feed Law of 1973, Chapter 106, Article 31C. Prepared fish bait distributed for recreational angling is not "commercial feed" under this statute. "Feed" means materials intended for nutritional consumption by animals. A dip bait or sponge bait is designed to attract fish through scent — not to nutritionally sustain them. Kansas K.A.R. 115-17-3 explicitly exempts "nonliving, commercially packaged fish bait." Major prepared bait manufacturers operate without state commercial feed registration.

Confirmation call: NCDA&CS Food & Drug Protection Division, (984) 236-4820. Ask: "Does prepared fish bait manufactured for recreational use fall under NC Commercial Feed Law registration requirements?" Document the response in writing. 20 minutes closes this permanently.

2 — FDA Food Facility Registration — Does Not Apply

Statute: 21 CFR 1.227; FFDCA Section 201(f). FDA food facility registration applies to facilities that manufacture, process, pack, or hold food for human or animal consumption. The statutory definition of "food" under Section 201(f) is "articles used for food or drink for man or other animals." The key phrase: "used for food." Bait is used to attract, not consumed as food. Established bait manufacturers operate across the country without FDA food facility registration. If FDA considered fish bait animal food, the industry would have been compelled to register after FSMA took effect in 2011.

Edge case to note: If a future product were developed as actual catfish feed for pond owners stocking private waters, that product would require registration. Pro Series catfish bait for recreational anglers is not that product.

3 — NC Marine Fisheries Fish Dealer Licenses — Does Not Apply

Relevant statutes: NCGS § 113-169.3 (Fish Dealer License), Standard Commercial Fishing License, Recreational Commercial Gear License, Permit to Introduce/Transfer/Hold Imported Marine and Estuarine Organisms. NC Division of Marine Fisheries licenses regulate the harvest and commercial handling of live marine and estuarine organisms, and commercial fishermen. Rusty's product is a manufactured good — not a marine organism. None of these licenses apply to prepared bait manufacturing.

Section 07

Spill Response — Operational Requirements

Minor Spill Protocol (under approximately 1 liter)

Step 1 — Immediate Personal Protection

Stop the source if safe to do so. Don nitrile gloves, splash goggles, and chemical-resistant apron before approaching. If any airborne vapor is present, add half-face respirator with organic vapor cartridge. Alert others to stay clear of the area.

Step 2 — Contain

Use absorbent booms or socks from the spill kit to contain lateral spread. Prevent from reaching floor drains. Do not use paper towels or combustibles as primary absorbent for formaldehyde solutions.

Step 3 — Neutralize

Apply sodium bisulfite (primary neutralizer) to the spilled material. Allow to react for 5–10 minutes. Sodium bisulfite reacts with formaldehyde to form a stable, less hazardous compound. Do not use bleach — the reaction generates chloromethyl ether, a known carcinogen.

Step 4 — Clean Up and Document

Collect all absorbed and neutralized material into a labeled waste container. Even neutralized spill cleanup material remains U122 hazardous waste and must be disposed of through the licensed hauler. Document the spill: date, time, estimated quantity, personnel involved, response actions taken. Retain documentation.

Spill Kit Contents — Required on Production Floor

  • Sodium bisulfite neutralizer (minimum 5 lbs)
  • Absorbent booms and socks — formaldehyde-compatible
  • Absorbent granules or pads (at least 1 bag)
  • Nitrile gloves — extra pairs (two sizes)
  • Splash goggles — two pairs
  • Sealed waste disposal bags/container with hazardous waste labels
  • Written spill response SOP laminated inside the kit

Pre-assembled commercial formaldehyde spill kits are available for $80–150. Recommended — faster than assembling components and ensures correct materials are present.

When to Handle In-House vs. Escalate

Handle in-house: Small splash on work surface, limited spread, contained to production area, no floor drain exposure, vapors manageable with ventilation and PPE, under approximately 1 liter.

Escalate immediately: Spill reaches floor drain, heavy vapor generation, over approximately 1 gallon, significant employee exposure symptoms, spill cannot be fully contained with on-hand materials.

NC NCDEQ 24-Hour Spill Hotline

1-800-858-0368 — Legal requirement to report releases that may impact the environment or reach waters of the state. When in doubt, call. Failure to report is its own violation.

Neutralized spill cleanup material is still U122 hazardous waste. Do not dispose of it in regular trash or down the drain. Collect in labeled HDPE containers and include in the next scheduled hazardous waste pickup.

Section A

Interim Operating Strategy

This section documents the interim operating posture during the period between production restart and permanent facility acquisition. It is written with candor about tradeoffs and accepted risks. It is not a statement of long-term operating philosophy.

Rusty's will restart production at the current interim facility while searching for a permanent, properly zoned and classified space. This is a deliberate business decision made with open eyes. The goal of this section is to define exactly what that posture means: what is being done right, what risk is being accepted knowingly, and why the balance is defensible on a defined timeline.

Core principle: Full physical compliance. Selective administrative registration. Every risk named explicitly.

A.1 — How OSHA and Regulators Actually Find Out

Understanding regulatory discovery mechanisms is essential for honest risk calibration. There are two categories: automatic external visibility and self-initiated requirements.

Automatic external visibility: EPA ID filing (required and creates a public record), workers' compensation filings (trigger insurance audits), building permits (visible to county), incident reports (any injury requiring medical attention beyond first aid must be reported to OSHA), and tax filings that identify the business as a manufacturer.

Self-initiated requirements include OSHA initial monitoring, EPA ID registration, and medical surveillance. If none of these are filed, the business is invisible to regulators — but also noncompliant.

How OSHA finds small manufacturing operations:

  1. Employee complaints — the #1 source. A current or former employee can file a complaint with OSHA at any time. OSHA investigates all formal complaints regardless of employer size.
  2. Referrals — from other agencies, insurance companies, and workers' comp carriers during claims processing.
  3. Incidents — any workplace injury or illness requiring more than first aid creates a paper trail. Formaldehyde-related health symptoms are a direct referral trigger.
  4. Insurance audits — workers' comp carriers conduct audits that often surface compliance gaps. Gaps found during an audit can be referred to OSHA or used to deny claims.

A 3–4 person operation making fish bait in Buncombe County is not a high-priority target for proactive OSHA inspection. The realistic risk window is: an employee gets sick or files a complaint, a spill requires outside response, or an insurance audit uncovers gaps. Sound physical practices reduce the probability of all three. This is the bet being made.

A.2 — EPA ID: The Corrected Picture

Earlier versions of this analysis stated that VSQGs are required to obtain an EPA ID number. The corrected picture, based on primary NCDEQ guidance:

What primary sources actually say: Under the 2016 RCRA Generator Improvements Rule, VSQGs are not federally required to obtain an EPA ID. However, NCDEQ recommends it, and practically speaking, licensed hazardous waste haulers in NC will not accept waste from a generator without an EPA ID number on file. The requirement is operational rather than statutory for VSQGs.

Practical conclusion: File for the EPA ID regardless. The filing is free, takes two days via RCRAInfo (rcrainfo.epa.gov), uses Form 8700-12, and creates a documented compliance record. Contact: Andrew Minter, NCDEQ Hazardous Waste Section, 919-707-8265.

Confirmed facts: NC EPA ID numbers use the NCD format prefix. No filing fee. The ID stays with the facility, not the operator. Contact Andrew Minter at NCDEQ before filing to confirm the current submission process.

A.3 — What Physical Compliance Looks Like

Everything below must be in place before production resumes. These protect the people in the building — that is the primary reason they exist, independent of any regulatory requirement.

1. Ventilation

Source-capture exhaust at formaldehyde mixing and addition points plus general dilution ventilation in the production area. Designed by a licensed NC mechanical engineer. Installation by Don's construction crew. Note: mechanical and electrical permits for ventilation installation are deferred during the interim period. This is an accepted gap — the risk of county discovery is low during a defined timeline. Physical function is not deferred: ventilation must work before production starts.

2. PPE — Stocked Before Day 1

  • Nitrile gloves (two sizes minimum)
  • Splash goggles (ANSI Z87.1 rated)
  • Chemical-resistant apron
  • Half-face respirator with OV/P100 combination cartridges
  • Extra PPE on hand — not just one set per person

3. Spill Kit

Dedicated formaldehyde spill kit on the production floor, fully stocked (see Section 7 for contents), with the written spill response SOP laminated inside. Pre-assembled kits available for $80–150. No reason to delay this purchase.

4. Eyewash Station

Non-negotiable. Required by ANSI Z358.1. Must be within 10 seconds of any formaldehyde handling point. Also cheap — a plumbed unit is $800–1,500; a self-contained gravity-fed unit is $150–300 as an interim measure if plumbing isn't available immediately. It's also cheap. No reason to skip it.

5. Hazardous Waste Accumulation

Two 5-gallon HDPE containers with tight-fitting lids, labeled with "Hazardous Waste," waste code U122, and hazard class. Stored in secondary containment tray. Weekly inspection log posted on the container (date, inspector initials, condition). Quarterly scheduled pickup with licensed hauler. The entire accumulation setup costs under $200 to establish.

6. Written Programs (Internal)

These live in a binder in the production area. They cost nothing to produce. Required programs:

  • OSHA Formaldehyde Protection Program (29 CFR 1910.1048 is the template)
  • Hazard Communication Plan (29 CFR 1910.1200)
  • Emergency Action Plan — evacuation routes, assembly point, emergency contacts
  • Spill Response SOP
  • Batch Production SOP

7. Employee Health Baseline

Baseline occupational health physicals for all team members before first formaldehyde exposure. $150–300 per person at an occupational medicine clinic. Annual follow-up thereafter. Creates a documented pre-exposure baseline that protects both the employee and the business in any future health claim.

A.6 — The Financial Case for Moving Quickly

Every month of interim operations at the current facility is a month of carrying formaldehyde compliance overhead that disappears at a properly zoned and classified permanent facility — and a month of living with the zoning risk that sits above every other question in this document. The compliance build-out at a permanent EMP- or OU-zoned facility resolves the zoning risk permanently, allows proper permits to be pulled and inspections to be passed, and creates a foundation that doesn't require managing a risk register.

The cost envelope for permanent-facility compliance is identical to the interim-facility numbers above — the money gets spent either way. The difference is that at the permanent facility, the compliance investment creates a durable, fully permitted asset rather than a managed-risk interim situation.

The interim posture is defensible for a defined timeline. The timeline matters. The faster Don identifies and moves to a permanent, properly zoned facility, the shorter the window of accumulated interim risk — and the sooner the compliance infrastructure can be built to last.
