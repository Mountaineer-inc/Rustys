Section 02

Zoning and Land Use — The Single Most Important Question

Priority — Resolve Before Any Other Regulatory Spend

This is the single most important compliance question before production restart. The Month 1 call to Planning at (828) 250-4830 takes priority over all other regulatory steps.

The Finding

The property is zoned CS (Commercial Service) / Multi-Use Commercial under Buncombe County zoning. Per Buncombe County Code of Ordinances, Chapter 78, Section 78-641, Table 1 (Permitted Use Table), manufacturing and processing operations are not a permitted use in the CS district.

Per Section 78-640(f), CS is designed for "Clustered commercial development… commercial activity in those specified areas with access to major traffic arteries." CS is intended for retail, offices, restaurants, and service-oriented commercial — not production operations.

The ordinance permits manufacturing only in EMP (Employment), AI (Airport Industry), and OU (Open Use) districts.

This is not a gray area. CS and manufacturing are incompatible uses under the current ordinance text.

Citation

Buncombe County Code of Ordinances, Chapter 78, Section 78-641, Table 1 — Permitted Use Table.
Available at: buncombecounty.org/common/planning/zoning/zoning-ordinance.pdf

Three Paths to Resolve

Path 1 — Grandfathered Nonconforming Use (most likely path if prior manufacturing was active)

If Rusty's was operating at this address under prior ownership with an active manufacturing use before the current zoning took effect — or before the area was rezoned around it — the property may have "grandfathered" nonconforming use status. This is common with older industrial buildings in corridors later rezoned commercial.

Key questions to ask Planning:

  • Does this parcel have nonconforming use status on record for manufacturing or industrial activity?
  • Does nonconforming status transfer with acquisition, or does it attach only to the prior operator?
  • What is the abandonment rule? Nonconforming uses typically lapse if discontinued for 6–12 months. If the prior owner ceased manufacturing before the sale closed, the status may already be lost.
  • Would any expansion or modification of the manufacturing use trigger loss of nonconforming status?

If the answer is yes: get it in writing. Confirm transfer rules and the abandonment clock.

Path 2 — Rezoning to EMP or OU

Formal process through the Planning Board and County Commissioners. Takes months, not days. Not guaranteed. Adjacent zoning patterns, neighbor sentiment, and staff recommendation all affect the outcome. Not the first path to pursue — but the right one if nonconforming status doesn't exist and the location is fixed.

Path 3 — No Clear Path

A variance is unlikely — variances address dimensional hardships, not fundamental use changes. A Conditional Use Permit appears unavailable — the ordinance does not list manufacturing as a conditional use in CS (no "C" in Table 1 for that use category). If neither nonconforming status nor rezoning is available, the location question goes back to Don before more capital is committed.

Month 1 Critical Action

Call — 15 Minutes — Free

Buncombe County Planning: (828) 250-4830

Ask directly: "What is the current zoning of [property address], and does the property have any nonconforming use status on record for manufacturing or industrial activity?"

If yes (nonconforming status confirmed): get it in writing. Confirm transfer rules and abandonment period. If no: the conversation about rezoning versus an alternative location happens before any further compliance or equipment spending.

The Stakes

If the property does not have grandfathered nonconforming manufacturing status, the business cannot legally operate as a manufacturer at that address under current zoning. The "owned building" advantage — which saves an estimated $18–24K/year in rent and is a material driver of Year 1 P&L — may not be usable for manufacturing without rezoning or a confirmed nonconforming designation.

Resolve this before committing additional capital to equipment move, compliance setup, or recipe verification.

Section 03

OSHA Formaldehyde Standard — 29 CFR 1910.1048

The most operationally consequential regulation we face. Most compliance is DIY-capable — only air monitoring, medical surveillance, and fit-testing require contractors. Sourced directly from the eCFR and OSHA enforcement materials.

Required Items: What the Regulation Actually Mandates

Item Required? How to Comply
Initial exposure monitoring (air sampling) Required Contracted — industrial hygienist with calibrated equipment and accredited lab analysis. One-time initial, then every 6 months if at or above Action Level. ~$1,500–3,000 initial.
Written compliance program Required if at or above 0.5 ppm DIY — 29 CFR 1910.1048 is the template. Read the regulation, write the program, follow it. No consultant required.
Medical surveillance (exams, questionnaires) Required for qualifying employees Contracted — occupational health provider. ~$150–300 per employee per year.
Hazard communication (SDS, labels) Required DIY — SDS sheets from suppliers, GHS-compliant container labeling.
Training (initial + annual) Required DIY — OSHA provides free training materials at osha.gov.
Respiratory protection program (if engineering controls insufficient) Required if applicable DIY written program. Respirator fit-testing contracted (~$75–150 per employee).
Ventilation engineering design Not required to hire; controls must be sufficient One-time mechanical engineer engagement is worth it for initial design (~$1,500–4,000). After that, DIY maintenance.
Full EHS consultant on retainer Recommended — Not Required OSHA doesn't require it. Firms sell this. If Joe and the Operations Manager can read 29 CFR 1910.1048 and follow it, they can comply directly. See NC DOL free alternative below.

The Free Alternative: NC Department of Labor Consultation

Free · Confidential · No Citations

NC Department of Labor, OSH Consultative Services: (919) 807-2899

NC DOL runs a free, confidential consultation program for small employers. No fines, no citations — purely advisory. They visit the facility, review the setup, identify compliance gaps, and provide a written punch list. This replaces roughly 80% of what a paid EHS consultant would do.

Schedule this as a top-priority Week 1–2 action, before production restarts.

Initial Monitoring

Every employer with formaldehyde exposure must conduct initial exposure monitoring within first operations. Conducted by an industrial hygienist with passive diffusion badges or active sampling.

Exception: If formaldehyde mixtures are ≤0.1% concentration AND exposure is documented below 0.1 ppm AND no employee health complaints, monitoring is not required.

Practical Reality for Rusty's

Rusty's formaldehyde solutions exceed the 0.1% exception threshold. We need to monitor. Initial monitoring must be conducted within 30 days of production start.

Periodic Monitoring

  • Every 6 months if exposure is at or above Action Level (0.5 ppm)
  • Annually if above STEL
  • Can reduce frequency after two consecutive samples below Action Level

Hierarchy of Controls (Required by OSHA)

  1. Substitution with less hazardous materials
  2. Engineering controls (ventilation, enclosed processes) — primary expected control
  3. Administrative controls
  4. PPE (respirators) — last resort, not a substitute

Engineering Controls Specifically Required

  • Local exhaust ventilation at emission points (mixing stations, formaldehyde addition)
  • General dilution ventilation to reduce ambient concentration
  • Enclosed processes where feasible
  • Air changes per hour sufficient to maintain below Action Level

Respiratory Protection Program — 29 CFR 1910.134

Triggered if:

  • Engineering/work practice controls alone cannot reduce exposure below PEL
  • During installation or repair of engineering controls
  • Emergency situations
  • Employee voluntarily requests protection

Medical Surveillance

Required if employee is exposed at or above Action Level (0.5 ppm):

  • Baseline exam, annual exams thereafter
  • Examination within 24 hours of any emergency exposure
  • Medical removal protection if physician determines health impairment

Training Requirements

At least annually for employees exposed at or above 0.1 ppm. Free OSHA training materials available at osha.gov — this is DIY:

  • Health effects of formaldehyde
  • Control measures
  • Safe work practices
  • Emergency procedures
  • Signs and symptoms of overexposure

Regulated Areas

Where exposure may exceed PEL or STEL — must be marked with signs bearing:

DANGER — FORMALDEHYDE — MAY CAUSE CANCER — CAUSES SKIN, EYE, AND RESPIRATORY IRRITATION — AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL ONLY

Recordkeeping

  • Exposure records: 30 years
  • Medical records: duration of employment + 30 years
  • Respirator fit testing: until replaced
  • Training records: duration of employment

Hazard Communication — 29 CFR 1910.1200

  • SDS (Safety Data Sheets) for every formaldehyde product — accessible to workers at all times
  • Labels per GHS standards
  • Chemical inventory list maintained

Emergency Procedures

  • Spill response plan with appropriate neutralizer (sodium sulfite or sodium bisulfite commonly recommended)
  • Eyewash stations and safety showers within 10 seconds' travel time (ANSI Z358.1)
  • Written evacuation procedures

What This Looks Like for Rusty's

  • Dedicated formaldehyde storage and mixing area with source-capture exhaust
  • Nitrile gloves, splash goggles, chemical apron at mixing stations
  • Written OSHA Formaldehyde Protection Program DIY — 29 CFR 1910.1048 is the template
  • Initial monitoring within 30 days of production start (contracted — industrial hygienist)
  • SDS binder accessible to all staff DIY
  • Warning signage posted DIY
  • Eyewash/shower station installed to ANSI Z358.1
  • Annual medical surveillance for Operations Manager and regularly exposed associates (contracted — occupational health provider)
  • Spill kit with sodium bisulfite on hand
  • Annual training documented DIY — free OSHA materials
Estimated First-Year OSHA Compliance Cost — Required Items Only

$2,500 – $5,500

Initial air monitoring ($1,500–3,000, contracted) + medical surveillance for qualifying employees ($400–1,000, contracted) + respirator fit-testing if needed ($75–150/person). Written programs and training are DIY at near-zero cost. Ventilation is a facility cost covered separately.

Section 04

Youth Employment — Easton's Scope of Work

Easton is 16, part-time W2, owner's son. NC and federal law impose real restrictions that are different — and more specific — than a typical teen job, because formaldehyde is in the building.

Required — Before His First Day

A Youth Employment Certificate must be obtained from the NC Department of Labor before Easton begins work. Required by NCGS § 95-25.5. A written job description documenting his Zone A scope and all prohibited tasks must also be in place before Day 1.

Applicable Law

  • NC Wage and Hour Act (NCGS § 95-25.5) — governs minors 14–17 in NC; Youth Employment Certificate required before work begins, hour restrictions, prohibited occupations list
  • FLSA Hazardous Orders (HO 1–17) — federal prohibitions on hazardous work for minors under 18; administered by the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division
  • NC Detrimental Occupations List — NC-specific restrictions beyond FLSA that apply to 16–17 year olds under NC DOL authority
  • OSHA 29 CFR 1910.1048 — formaldehyde standard that defines the action level (0.5 ppm) and governs who may be present in regulated production areas

NC Hour Rules for 16–17 Year Olds

  • No federal or NC daily or weekly hour cap for 16–17 (unlike 14–15 year olds, who have stricter limits)
  • Cannot work between 11:00 PM and 5:00 AM on school nights without written consent from parent and school principal — NCGS § 95-25.5(c)
  • Given Easton's 10–15 hr school year schedule, this is not a practical constraint — but it must be documented if he ever works late on a school night

Zone Framework — Clean vs. Production

The facility operates on a two-zone model for Easton's scope. The Operations Manager is responsible for maintaining physical zone separation and ensuring Easton is never present in Zone B during active production. This is not Easton's responsibility to manage — it is a supervision and facility management obligation.

Zone A — Clean / Administrative

Office, warehouse storage, order fulfillment, packaging of sealed goods, content creation. No formaldehyde handling at any point.

  • Office and administrative work
  • Order processing and fulfillment
  • Packaging and labeling (sealed finished goods only)
  • Inventory counting and FIFO rotation (dry goods only)
  • Facility organization and cleaning (non-production areas)
  • Photo/video content creation
  • Marketing and social media support
  • Product field testing (fishing trips with finished product)
  • Learning rotations — observation only
Zone B — Production / Regulated

Active formaldehyde production, mixing, and batch operations. Easton is not permitted in this zone during active production under any circumstance.

  • Formaldehyde handling — prohibited
  • Active batch production — prohibited
  • Chemical mixing station area — prohibited
  • Waste handling and disposal — prohibited
  • Forklift / Komatsu FG25C operation — prohibited
  • Confined space entry — prohibited
  • Any respirator-required task — prohibited

  • Vehicle operation — permitted if: valid NC license, trips within 25-mile radius, vehicle under 6,000 lbs GVW, no towing, driver's ed certificate on file, written driving policy signed by employee and employer. FLSA HO #2 governs minor motor vehicle operation.
  • Small hand-operated tools — permitted in non-production areas for packaging, maintenance, and organization. Keep to tasks clearly outside the production process; avoid power tools in gray-zone contexts.
  • Being in Zone B when production is fully inactive — when no active formaldehyde work is underway and the area has been cleared, brief supervised entry for observation or general facility tasks is not prohibited. When active production is running: Zone A only.

Before Day 1 — Required Documentation Checklist

  • Youth Employment Certificate — obtain from NC DOL Wage and Hour Bureau before first day. Required by NCGS § 95-25.5. Contact: 1-800-625-2267. Cannot be obtained retroactively.
  • Written job description — Easton's scope of work in writing, specifying Zone A activities, the full prohibition list above with citations, and supervision requirements. Signed by Don.
  • Signed acknowledgment (Easton + Don) — documents that Easton has been briefed on zone rules and his specific restrictions.
  • OSHA formaldehyde hazard awareness training — even though Easton doesn't handle formaldehyde, general chemical hazard awareness is appropriate and creates a defensible training record.
  • Driving authorization documents (if applicable) — copy of NC license, driver's ed certificate, signed driving policy.
  • Training records file created — maintained per standard employment practice for duration of employment.
Why the Zone Structure Works

Most of what Easton is genuinely good at — content creation, marketing support, order fulfillment, product field testing — is squarely in Zone A anyway. The legal framework doesn't conflict with the role design; it requires that Zone B stays physically separate and that the separation is documented. A well-maintained zone boundary protects the business, gives Easton a clear scope with real accountability, and makes the employment arrangement fully auditable if a labor audit ever occurs.

Zone enforcement is an Operations Manager responsibility, not Easton's. Build it into facility procedure before he starts.

Section 08

NC Commercial Feed Law — Probably Does Not Apply

Finding: Does Not Apply

Statute: NCGS § 106-284.33(4) — NC Commercial Feed Law of 1973, Chapter 106, Article 31C.

Prepared fish bait distributed for recreational angling is not "commercial feed" under this statute. Fish bait is designed to attract fish for capture, not to nutritionally sustain them. One confirmation call to NCDA&CS at (984) 236-4820 closes this permanently.

Sourced directly from NCGS Chapter 106, Article 31C (NC Commercial Feed Law of 1973).

The Statutory Definition

§ 106-284.33(4): "The term 'commercial feed' means all materials, except whole unmixed seed such as corn... which are distributed for use as feed or for mixing in feed..."

Why Fish Bait Isn't "Commercial Feed"

"Feed" in agricultural and regulatory context means materials intended for nutritional consumption by animals. A prepared dip bait, stink bait, or sponge bait is designed to:

  • Attract fish through scent and taste
  • Stick to a hook
  • Release attractants in water

It is NOT designed to nutritionally sustain fish or be consumed as a complete or supplemental diet. This matches how AAFCO (Association of American Feed Control Officials, whose definitions NC adopts) treats the distinction.

Industry Practice Confirms This

Major prepared fish bait manufacturers (Sonny's/Triple S by Hootman Brothers in Kansas, Magic Bait in Oklahoma, Team Catfish by Doc Lange in Georgia, Catfish Charlie) do not register products under state commercial feed laws. They operate as manufacturers of fishing tackle/bait products, not feed manufacturers.

Other States Explicitly Exempt Prepared Bait

Kansas regulation (K.A.R. 115-17-3) explicitly exempts "nonliving, commercially packaged fish bait" from its commercial fish bait permit system. The principle is consistent across states.

Recommended Verification

Action — 20 Minutes

NCDA&CS Food & Drug Protection Division: (984) 236-4820

Ask specifically: "Does prepared fish bait manufactured for recreational use fall under NC Commercial Feed Law registration requirements, or is it outside that regulatory framework?"

Document the response in writing. Get the name and title of whoever we speak with. This is a 20-minute task that eliminates the question permanently.

Estimated cost if somehow it does apply: Worst case: annual registration at $40/SKU × 5–10 SKUs = $200–$400/year. Manageable even if it applies.

Section 09

FDA Food Facility Registration — Probably Does Not Apply

Finding: Does Not Apply

Statute: 21 CFR 1.227 — FDA Small Entity Compliance Guide (2018).

Prepared fish bait is not "food" under the FFDCA definition. Bait is used to attract fish for capture, not consumed as food or feed. Established bait manufacturers operate across states without FDA animal food registration. The statutory reading is clear; no registration action required.

Sourced directly from 21 CFR 1.227 and FDA Small Entity Compliance Guide (2018).

What the Regulation Covers

FDA food facility registration applies to facilities that "manufacture, process, pack, or hold food for human or animal consumption." The "food" definition at 21 CFR 1.227 includes fruits, vegetables, fish, dairy, animal feed (including pet food), food and feed ingredients, dietary supplements, bakery goods, and canned foods. It explicitly excludes food contact substances and pesticides. Fish bait is not mentioned.

The FFDCA Statutory Definition

Section 201(f) of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act defines "food" as "articles used for food or drink for man or other animals." The key phrase: "used for food." Bait is used to attract, not to be consumed as food. This is the same distinction that excludes fish bait from NC Commercial Feed Law.

Industry Practice Confirms

Prepared bait manufacturers operate without FDA food facility registration. If FDA considered fish bait animal food, the industry would have been compelled to register after FSMA took effect in 2011, and they would publicly discuss compliance. They don't.

One Edge Case to Note

Future Product Caution

If we ever developed a product intended as actual catfish feed — for example, for catfish pond owners stocking private waters — that product would be commercial feed and would require NC registration and FDA facility registration. Pro Series catfish bait for recreational anglers is not that product.

Verification (Optional)

FDA's Industry Systems portal (fda.gov/furls) handles registration questions. Email FURLS@fda.gov to confirm if desired. Not required — statutory reading is clear.

Section 10

NC Marine Fisheries — Does Not Apply

Finding: Does Not Apply

NC Division of Marine Fisheries licenses regulate the harvest and commercial handling of live marine and estuarine organisms. Rusty's product is a manufactured good, not a marine organism. None of these licenses apply to prepared bait manufacturing.

Relevant statutes reviewed and confirmed inapplicable: NCGS § 113-169.3 (Fish Dealer License), Standard Commercial Fishing License, Recreational Commercial Gear License, Permit to Introduce/Transfer/Hold Imported Marine and Estuarine Organisms.

Licenses Reviewed and Confirmed Inapplicable

  • Fish Dealer License (NCGS § 113-169.3) — for dealing in raw fish or live bait
  • Standard Commercial Fishing License — for commercial fishermen
  • Recreational Commercial Gear License — for recreational use of commercial-type gear
  • Permit to Introduce, Transfer, or Hold Imported Marine and Estuarine Organisms — for moving live organisms

None of these apply to prepared bait manufacturing. The NC Division of Marine Fisheries regulates the harvest and commercial handling of live marine and estuarine organisms. Our product is a manufactured good, not a marine organism.

Section 11

Facility Build-Out Standards

Because we're not regulated as a food or feed facility, the strict NSF/ANSI, USDA, and Grade A Dairy-style construction standards do not apply.

Required by OSHA (Worker Safety, Not Product Safety)

  • Adequate ventilation (engineering controls for formaldehyde)
  • Eyewash station and safety shower to ANSI Z358.1
  • Emergency lighting and egress per NC Fire Code
  • Warning signage in regulated areas
  • Shatter-resistant lighting over any area with product exposure

Required by NC Building Code F-1 Classification

  • Standard commercial construction
  • Wall/floor/ceiling fire ratings per Chapter 6
  • Sprinklers if building exceeds 12,000 sq ft (F-1 threshold)
  • Code-compliant egress per Chapter 10

Required by Buncombe County Fire Code

  • Chemical storage per NC Fire Code (based on IFC)
  • NFPA hazard diamond signage on storage areas
  • Proper electrical classification in chemical use/storage areas (may not require explosion-proof at our quantities)

Not Strictly Required — But Smart Practice

  • FRP (Fiberglass Reinforced Panel) walls: Not required for fish bait. FRP in mixing and production zones is still worth doing — $8–$15/sq ft installed in wet-process zones means ~$2,000–$4,000 for a focused installation. Makes cleaning easier, signals professionalism during any inspection, prevents moisture damage to drywall.
  • Floor drains to grease trap: Not required unless discharging food-grade wastewater. For formaldehyde-contaminated rinse water, collect to hazardous waste stream — not sewer discharge.
  • Separate handwashing sink: Not federally mandated, but OSHA requires handwashing facilities for hygiene. Good practice at minimal cost.
  • Stainless steel food-contact surfaces: Not required. Food-grade plastic containers, properly cleanable painted or FRP surfaces are adequate.

Ventilation — The Real Build Cost

Source-capture exhaust at formaldehyde mixing points plus general dilution ventilation in the production area. Designed by a licensed mechanical engineer.

Typical System Cost for Our Scale

$8,000 – $18,000 installed

Specs we'd want:

  • Source-capture hood over mixing/addition stations
  • Downdraft or side-draft capture at open containers
  • General exhaust to achieve 10–12 air changes per hour in the production area
  • Make-up air to replace exhausted air
  • Exhaust discharge located away from building air intakes and property lines

Insurance Requirements

Not a regulatory requirement but financially essential:

  • General Liability: $1M/$2M minimum
  • Product Liability: $1M minimum (critical — even if not intended for human consumption, kids and pets could access)
  • Workers' Compensation: mandatory in NC for 3+ employees
  • Commercial Property: facility and equipment
  • Pollution Liability: recommended ($1,500–$3,000/year for $500K–$1M coverage)

Expected total annual premiums: $4,000–$8,000

Section 16

What I'm Still Uncertain About

Being direct about remaining questions — what isn't resolved and what resolves it.

  • Zoning / nonconforming use status — the most significant open question. Does the property have grandfathered nonconforming manufacturing status under Buncombe County Zoning? Does it transfer with acquisition? Resolved by the Month 1 call to Planning at (828) 250-4830, not by more research.
  • Change of Occupancy review requirement depends on the building's previously-certified use. If the prior owner operated under Factory Industrial F-1 and their Certificate of Occupancy was current, we may have less scrutiny. If the building was previously a different use class, full review is likely. Confirm during the Planning call.
  • NCDA&CS Commercial Feed classification — the read is clear that fish bait isn't feed under NCGS § 106-284.33(4), but a confirmation call to (984) 236-4820 is trivially easy and eliminates all doubt.
  • Interstate sales regulations — if we sell into states with more aggressive prepared-bait classification, we may need state-specific compliance. No state reviewed classifies prepared bait as feed, but there are 50 states. This is a Year 2+ question when expansion happens.
  • Specific formaldehyde SDS and concentration for the inventory we're acquiring from the seller. Different concentrations change PPE requirements, ventilation sizing, and MAQ calculations. Pull SDS sheets as part of acquisition diligence — no cost.
Resolution Path

Zoning: one free phone call. Change of Occupancy: confirmed during Planning call. Commercial Feed: one free phone call. Interstate sales: defer to Year 2. SDS sheets: pull during acquisition diligence at no cost.

All open uncertainties have clear, affordable resolution paths. None are deal-breakers — but the zoning question must be answered first.
