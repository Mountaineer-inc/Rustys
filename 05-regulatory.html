Regulatory Compliance

What applies, what doesn't, and what to do before production restart

Prepared for Joseph Skinner
Updated April 2026
Status For review and action
Section 01

Executive Summary

Three real layers and one ambiguous one — here is the complete regulatory picture before production restarts.

My first regulatory document was cautious to the point of including things that probably don't apply. After going directly to the statutes and regulations, here's what's actually different:

  • NC Commercial Feed registration almost certainly does NOT apply to prepared fish bait. The statute defines "commercial feed" as materials "distributed for use as feed or for mixing in feed" (NCGS § 106-284.33(4)). Fish bait is distributed to attract fish for harvest, not to feed them nutritionally. Still worth confirming with NCDA&CS, but don't budget for this.
  • FDA food facility registration almost certainly does NOT apply. 21 CFR 1.227 defines food for registration purposes, and fish bait is not on the list. Established prepared bait manufacturers operate across states without FDA animal food registration because bait isn't animal food.
  • NC Marine Fisheries "Fish Dealer" licenses DO NOT apply. These regulate live bait dealers and commercial fishermen, not prepared bait manufacturers.
  • What's replacing those concerns is real and larger: NC Building Code occupancy classification. Formaldehyde use in a production facility can trigger Group H (High Hazard) classification rather than Factory Industrial F-1, depending on quantities on site. This has real construction implications.

The Four Layers

Real Layer 1 — Federal OSHA Formaldehyde Standard (29 CFR 1910.1048). The single most consequential regulation we face. Engineering controls, monitoring, medical surveillance, training, signage. No question it applies.

Real Layer 2 — Federal EPA RCRA Hazardous Waste. Formaldehyde waste is federally classified as hazardous (EPA code U122 under 40 CFR 261.33). Administered in NC by NCDEQ Division of Waste Management Hazardous Waste Section. Compliance is procedural but non-optional.

Real Layer 3 — Buncombe County Building Code and Occupancy Classification. The NC Building Code (based on IBC) classifies the facility as either Factory Industrial F-1 or Group H depending on formaldehyde quantities. A Change of Occupancy review may be required since the building is being used for a different purpose than its last certified occupancy.

Ambiguous Layer — NC Commercial Feed and FDA Animal Food. Based on statutory reading, neither applies. But we should make one confirmation call to NCDA&CS to put this in writing. 20 minutes of work, eliminates the question forever.

Worker safety is the most operationally consequential regulation we face. Engineering controls, monitoring, medical surveillance, training, signage. No question it applies.
Three free calls — eliminate most uncertainty

(1) Call NCDA&CS at (984) 236-4820 — confirm prepared fish bait is not Commercial Feed. 20 minutes, in writing, permanently resolved.
(2) Call Buncombe County Planning & Development — verify zoning and Change of Occupancy status.
(3) Call Buncombe County Fire Marshal at (828) 250-6620 — free pre-consultation on chemical storage layout.

Between these three calls and a $1,500–4,000 engineer analysis, we eliminate the biggest open questions in this document.

The non-negotiable recommendation: Hire a NC environmental health and safety consultant and/or licensed mechanical engineer for a pre-launch walkthrough. $1,500–3,500. Combined with a Buncombe County Fire Marshal pre-consultation (free), this gives us a defensible compliance posture before we restart production.

The most operationally consequential regulation we face. Sourced directly from the eCFR and OSHA enforcement materials.

Real-world enforcement context: A NC cabinet manufacturer was fined $47,000 after air sampling showed 1.2 ppm average. Remediation cost $180,000 in ventilation and reformulation. This is the kind of operation OSHA actively inspects.

Initial Monitoring

Every employer with formaldehyde exposure must conduct initial exposure monitoring within first operations. Can be done by industrial hygienist with passive diffusion badges or active sampling.

Exception: If formaldehyde mixtures are ≤0.1% concentration AND exposure is documented below 0.1 ppm AND no employee health complaints, monitoring is not required.

Practical Reality for Rusty's

Rusty's formaldehyde solutions exceed the 0.1% exception threshold. We need to monitor. Initial monitoring must be conducted within 30 days of production start.

Periodic Monitoring

  • Every 6 months if exposure is at or above Action Level (0.5 ppm)
  • Annually if above STEL
  • Can reduce frequency after two consecutive samples below Action Level

Hierarchy of Controls (Required by OSHA)

  1. Substitution with less hazardous materials
  2. Engineering controls (ventilation, enclosed processes) — primary expected control
  3. Administrative controls
  4. PPE (respirators) — last resort, not a substitute

Engineering Controls Specifically Required

  • Local exhaust ventilation at emission points (mixing stations, formaldehyde addition)
  • General dilution ventilation to reduce ambient concentration
  • Enclosed processes where feasible
  • Air changes per hour sufficient to maintain below Action Level

Respiratory Protection Program

29 CFR 1910.134 is triggered if:

  • Engineering/work practice controls alone cannot reduce exposure below PEL
  • During installation or repair of engineering controls
  • Emergency situations
  • Employee voluntarily requests protection

Medical Surveillance

Required if employee is exposed at or above Action Level (0.5 ppm):

  • Baseline exam, annual exams thereafter
  • Examination within 24 hours of any emergency exposure
  • Medical removal protection if physician determines health impairment

Training Requirements

At least annually for employees exposed at or above 0.1 ppm:

  • Health effects of formaldehyde
  • Control measures
  • Safe work practices
  • Emergency procedures
  • Signs and symptoms of overexposure

Regulated Areas

Where exposure may exceed PEL or STEL — must be marked with signs bearing:

DANGER — FORMALDEHYDE — MAY CAUSE CANCER — CAUSES SKIN, EYE, AND RESPIRATORY IRRITATION — AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL ONLY

Access limited to authorized persons. Respirator requirements enforced.

Recordkeeping

  • Exposure records: 30 years
  • Medical records: duration of employment + 30 years
  • Respirator fit testing: until replaced
  • Training records: duration of employment

Hazard Communication — 29 CFR 1910.1200

  • SDS (Safety Data Sheets) for every formaldehyde product
  • Accessible to workers at all times
  • Labels per GHS standards
  • Chemical inventory list maintained

Emergency Procedures

  • Spill response plan with appropriate neutralizer (sodium sulfite or sodium bisulfite commonly recommended)
  • Eyewash stations and safety showers within 10 seconds' travel time (ANSI Z358.1)
  • Written evacuation procedures

What This Looks Like for Rusty's

  • Dedicated formaldehyde storage and mixing area with source-capture exhaust
  • Nitrile gloves, splash goggles, chemical apron at mixing stations
  • Written OSHA Formaldehyde Protection Program
  • Initial monitoring within 30 days of production start
  • SDS binder accessible to all staff
  • Warning signage posted
  • Eyewash/shower station installed to ANSI Z358.1
  • Annual medical surveillance for Production Lead and regularly exposed associates
  • Spill kit with sodium bisulfite on hand
  • Annual training documented
Estimated First-Year OSHA Compliance Cost

$4,000 – $8,000

Covers monitoring, ventilation, PPE, training, medical surveillance, eyewash station, and signage.

EPA RCRA Hazardous Waste — Confirmed Applies

Sourced from 40 CFR 261.33, EPA formaldehyde page, and NCDEQ Hazardous Waste Section.

Formaldehyde Is Federally Hazardous Waste

  • 40 CFR 261.33 classifies discarded commercial chemical products containing formaldehyde as hazardous (EPA code U122)
  • 40 CFR 116.4 — formaldehyde is a Clean Water Act designated hazardous substance
  • Clean Air Act Section 112 — formaldehyde is a Hazardous Air Pollutant (HAP)

NC DEQ Division of Waste Management Hazardous Waste Section administers federal RCRA regulations. NC has adopted federal generator categories and reporting requirements without adding state-specific waste codes.

Section 04

NC Building Code — Occupancy Classification Matters A Lot

The Critical Cost Variable

F-1 vs. Group H occupancy classification determines whether facility build-out costs $25K or $100K+. Resolve this first.

Sourced from NC State Building Code Chapter 3 (based on IBC). This is what the first document under-emphasized.

Group F-1 vs. Group H — The Critical Question

Factory Industrial F-1 (Moderate Hazard): Most manufacturing operations. Includes food processing, chemical blending below high-hazard thresholds, etc.

Group H (High Hazard): Operations involving the manufacturing, processing, generation or storage of materials constituting physical or health hazards in quantities exceeding the Maximum Allowable Quantity (MAQ) per control area, per Tables 307.1(1) and 307.1(2) of the NC Building Code.

Formaldehyde Under MAQ Tables

Formaldehyde (at concentrations typically used in bait) is both:

  • Toxic (Table 307.1(2) — H-4 Health Hazard)
  • Combustible/flammable depending on concentration (may affect H-2 or H-3 classification)

Maximum Allowable Quantity in a single control area (without sprinklers, no special increases):

  • Toxic liquid: ~500 lbs
  • With sprinklers and storage cabinets: can double
The Engineering Question

The 18 cases of formaldehyde we're acquiring could be just under or just over the MAQ threshold depending on concentration and container configuration. This is a licensed architect or mechanical engineer question, not a strategy document question.

Two Scenarios

Scenario A (Best Case) — Facility Stays Group F-1

  • Keep formaldehyde on-site below MAQ (may require external bulk storage or more frequent smaller deliveries)
  • Standard F-1 construction: typical wall/ceiling ratings, standard sprinklers if over 12,000 sq ft
  • Control area design separates formaldehyde storage from production

Scenario B — Facility Classified Group H

  • Much more restrictive construction requirements
  • Fire-rated separation from other occupancies
  • Required sprinkler system
  • Required emergency alarm system
  • May require spill containment, gas detection, explosion-proof electrical in some areas
  • Can add $30,000–$80,000 to build-out costs
Likely Outcome

With formaldehyde in sealed storage, proper control area design, and sprinklers, we can almost certainly stay in F-1 classification. But this needs to be designed in, not assumed.

Change of Occupancy Review

NC Existing Building Code Chapter 10 requires a Change of Occupancy review when a building's use changes from its last certified occupancy. If the previous use of the Buncombe County building was something other than chemical/feed manufacturing, we may trigger this review. It's not a deal-breaker, but it's a permitting step.

Action Items for Building Code Compliance

  1. Determine the building's currently-certified occupancy classification
  2. Determine the formaldehyde quantity plan (how much on-site at any time)
  3. Engage a licensed mechanical engineer or NC architect for occupancy analysis
  4. Apply for permits reflecting final classification
  5. Complete Change of Occupancy review if required

Estimated Cost

  • Engineer/architect analysis: $1,500–$4,000
  • Permits and Change of Occupancy review: $500–$1,500
  • Construction to meet F-1 requirements: likely already in operational budget
  • Construction to meet Group H requirements (if triggered): $30,000–$80,000 additional

Buncombe County Requirements

Sourced from Buncombe County Permits & Inspections, NC Building Code, and County zoning ordinances.

Zoning Verification — Do First

Before permits, confirm the building's zoning allows light manufacturing / factory industrial use.

  • If in unincorporated Buncombe County: Buncombe County Planning & Development
  • If within Asheville city limits: City of Asheville Planning Department (different jurisdiction)
  • Steep Slope Overlay or other overlays may add requirements

Key question: Is "factory industrial" use allowed by right at this parcel? If not, conditional use permit or variance needed.

Property in Open Use Zoning District with no overlays may not require a Zoning Permit. Most Buncombe County industrial-zoned parcels will require one.

Building Permits

Required when:

  • Any construction valued over $40,000 (NCGS Section 87-1)
  • Any load-bearing modifications
  • Plumbing design changes
  • Mechanical/HVAC additions or modifications
  • Electrical design changes

Ventilation system installation for formaldehyde will trigger mechanical and electrical permits. Combined cost of permits: $300–$800.

Fire Marshal — Pre-Consultation Recommended

Free Consultation — Schedule Early

Buncombe County Fire Marshal: (828) 250-6620

Bring: formaldehyde quantities planned on-site, storage layout sketch, SDS sheets, planned ventilation system description.

The Fire Marshal will identify: chemical storage room rating requirements, required NFPA hazard diamond signage, secondary containment (spill pallets) requirements, fire suppression requirements, and specific egress requirements.

Business Licensing

NC does not have a statewide business license. Buncombe County does not require one either for most operations. What we DO need:

  • Asheville Business Privilege License if within city limits (not required in unincorporated county)
  • NC Unemployment Insurance registration via NC Division of Employment Security (required for any employer)
  • NC Workers' Compensation coverage (required for 3+ employees under NCGS Chapter 97)

Section 06

NC Commercial Feed Law — Probably Does Not Apply

Finding: Does Not Apply

Statute: NCGS § 106-284.33(4) — NC Commercial Feed Law of 1973, Chapter 106, Article 31C.

Prepared fish bait distributed for recreational angling is not "commercial feed" under this statute. Fish bait is designed to attract fish for capture, not to nutritionally sustain them. One confirmation call to NCDA&CS at (984) 236-4820 closes this permanently.

Sourced directly from NCGS Chapter 106, Article 31C (NC Commercial Feed Law of 1973).

The Statutory Definition

§ 106-284.33(4): "The term 'commercial feed' means all materials, except whole unmixed seed such as corn... which are distributed for use as feed or for mixing in feed..."

Why Fish Bait Isn't "Commercial Feed"

"Feed" in agricultural and regulatory context means materials intended for nutritional consumption by animals. A prepared dip bait, stink bait, or sponge bait is designed to:

  • Attract fish through scent and taste
  • Stick to a hook
  • Release attractants in water

It is NOT designed to:

  • Nutritionally sustain fish
  • Be consumed as a complete or supplemental diet

This matches how AAFCO (Association of American Feed Control Officials, whose definitions NC adopts) treats the distinction. AAFCO covers "animal food" — defined as materials intended to provide "taste, aroma, or nutritive value." If a product's primary use is attraction for capture rather than nutrition, it's outside the feed framework.

Industry Practice Confirms This

Major prepared fish bait manufacturers (Sonny's/Triple S by Hootman Brothers in Kansas, Magic Bait in Oklahoma, Team Catfish by Doc Lange in Georgia, Catfish Charlie) do not register products under state commercial feed laws. They operate as manufacturers of fishing tackle/bait products, not feed manufacturers.

Other States Explicitly Exempt Prepared Bait

Kansas regulation (K.A.R. 115-17-3) explicitly exempts "nonliving, commercially packaged fish bait" from its commercial fish bait permit system. The principle is consistent across states.

Recommended Verification

A single phone call to NCDA&CS to put this in writing:

Action — 20 Minutes

NCDA&CS Food & Drug Protection Division: (984) 236-4820

Ask specifically: "Does prepared fish bait manufactured for recreational use fall under NC Commercial Feed Law registration requirements, or is it outside that regulatory framework?"

Document the response in writing. Get the name and title of whoever we speak with. This is a 20-minute task that eliminates the question permanently.

Estimated cost if somehow it does apply: Worst case: annual registration at $40/SKU × 5–10 SKUs = $200–$400/year. Manageable even if it applies.

Section 07

FDA Food Facility Registration — Probably Does Not Apply

Finding: Does Not Apply

Statute: 21 CFR 1.227 — FDA Small Entity Compliance Guide (2018).

Prepared fish bait is not "food" under the FFDCA definition. Bait is used to attract fish for capture, not consumed as food or feed. Established bait manufacturers operate across states without FDA animal food registration. The statutory reading is clear; no registration action required.

Sourced directly from 21 CFR 1.227 and FDA Small Entity Compliance Guide (2018).

What the Regulation Covers

FDA food facility registration applies to facilities that "manufacture, process, pack, or hold food for human or animal consumption." The "food" definition at 21 CFR 1.227 gives examples:

Included

  • Fruits, vegetables, fish, dairy, eggs
  • Animal feed (including pet food)
  • Food and feed ingredients
  • Dietary supplements
  • Bakery goods, snack foods, candy
  • Canned foods

Explicitly Not Included

  • Food contact substances
  • Pesticides

Not mentioned anywhere: Fish bait.

The FFDCA Statutory Definition

Section 201(f) of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act defines "food" as "articles used for food or drink for man or other animals." The key phrase: "used for food." Bait is used to attract, not to be consumed as food. This is the same distinction that excludes fish bait from NC Commercial Feed Law.

Industry Practice Confirms

Prepared bait manufacturers operate without FDA food facility registration. If FDA considered fish bait animal food, the industry would have been compelled to register after FSMA took effect in 2011, and they would publicly discuss compliance. They don't.

One Edge Case to Note

Future Product Caution

If we ever developed a product intended as actual catfish feed — for example, for catfish pond owners stocking private waters — that product would be commercial feed and would require NC registration and FDA facility registration. Pro Series catfish bait for recreational anglers is not that product.

Verification (Optional)

FDA's Industry Systems portal (fda.gov/furls) handles registration questions. Email FURLS@fda.gov to confirm if desired. Not required — statutory reading is clear.

NC Marine Fisheries — Does Not Apply

Finding: Does Not Apply

NC Division of Marine Fisheries licenses regulate the harvest and commercial handling of live marine and estuarine organisms. Rusty's product is a manufactured good, not a marine organism. None of these licenses apply to prepared bait manufacturing.

Relevant statutes reviewed and confirmed inapplicable: NCGS § 113-169.3 (Fish Dealer License), Standard Commercial Fishing License, Recreational Commercial Gear License, Permit to Introduce/Transfer/Hold Imported Marine and Estuarine Organisms.

Quickly disposed of because the name is confusing. The NC Division of Marine Fisheries regulates live organisms, not manufactured goods.

Licenses Reviewed and Confirmed Inapplicable

  • Fish Dealer License (NCGS § 113-169.3) — for dealing in raw fish or live bait
  • Standard Commercial Fishing License — for commercial fishermen
  • Recreational Commercial Gear License — for recreational use of commercial-type gear
  • Permit to Introduce, Transfer, or Hold Imported Marine and Estuarine Organisms — for moving live organisms

None of these apply to prepared bait manufacturing. The NC Division of Marine Fisheries regulates the harvest and commercial handling of live marine and estuarine organisms. Our product is a manufactured good, not a marine organism.

Section 09

Facility Build-Out Standards

Because we're not regulated as a food or feed facility, the strict NSF/ANSI, USDA, and Grade A Dairy-style construction standards do not apply.

Required by OSHA (Worker Safety, Not Product Safety)

  • Adequate ventilation (engineering controls for formaldehyde)
  • Eyewash station and safety shower to ANSI Z358.1
  • Emergency lighting and egress per NC Fire Code
  • Warning signage in regulated areas
  • Shatter-resistant lighting over any area with product exposure

Required by NC Building Code F-1 Classification

  • Standard commercial construction
  • Wall/floor/ceiling fire ratings per Chapter 6
  • Sprinklers if building exceeds 12,000 sq ft (F-1) or based on Group H triggers
  • Code-compliant egress per Chapter 10

Required by Buncombe County Fire Code

  • Chemical storage per NC Fire Code (based on IFC)
  • NFPA hazard diamond signage on storage areas
  • Proper electrical classification in chemical use/storage areas (may not require explosion-proof at our quantities)

Not Strictly Required — But Smart Practice

  • FRP (Fiberglass Reinforced Panel) walls: Not required for fish bait. Only mandated for Grade A dairy, USDA meat processing, and some human food categories. FRP in mixing and production zones is still worth doing — $8–$15/sq ft installed in wet-process zones means ~$2,000–$4,000 for a focused installation. Makes cleaning easier, signals professionalism during any inspection, prevents moisture damage to drywall.
  • Floor drains to grease trap or sanitary sewer pretreatment: Not required unless discharging food-grade wastewater. For formaldehyde-contaminated rinse water, the better approach is collection to hazardous waste stream, not sewer discharge.
  • Separate handwashing sink in processing area: Not federally mandated, but OSHA requires handwashing facilities for hygiene. A basic handwash sink in the production area is good practice at minimal cost.
  • Stainless steel food-contact surfaces: Not required. Food-grade plastic containers, properly cleanable painted or FRP surfaces are adequate.

Ventilation — The Real Build Cost

Source-capture exhaust at formaldehyde mixing points plus general dilution ventilation in the production area. Engineered by a licensed mechanical engineer.

Typical System Cost for Our Scale

$8,000 – $18,000 installed

Specs we'd want:

  • Source-capture hood over mixing/addition stations
  • Downdraft or side-draft capture at open containers
  • General exhaust to achieve 10–12 air changes per hour in the production area
  • Make-up air to replace exhausted air
  • Exhaust discharge located away from building air intakes and property lines

Insurance Requirements

Not a regulatory requirement but financially essential:

  • General Liability: $1M/$2M minimum
  • Product Liability: $1M minimum (critical — even if not intended for human consumption, kids and pets could access)
  • Workers' Compensation: mandatory in NC for 3+ employees
  • Commercial Property: facility and equipment
  • Pollution Liability: recommended ($1,500–$3,000/year for $500K–$1M coverage)

Expected total annual premiums: $4,000–$8,000

Section 14

What I'm Still Uncertain About

Being direct about remaining questions — what isn't resolved and what resolves it.

  • Exact Group F-1 vs. Group H classification requires a licensed engineer's analysis of formaldehyde quantities, container types, and control area design. Best case (F-1): standard construction. Worst case (Group H): +$30–$80K in build-out.
  • Change of Occupancy review requirement depends on the building's previously-certified use. If the prior owner operated under Factory Industrial F-1 and their Certificate of Occupancy was current, we may have less scrutiny. If the building was previously a different use class, full review is likely.
  • NCDA&CS Commercial Feed classification — the read is clear that fish bait isn't feed under NCGS § 106-284.33(4), but a confirmation call to (984) 236-4820 is trivially easy and eliminates all doubt.
  • Interstate sales regulations — if we sell into states with more aggressive prepared-bait classification, we may need state-specific compliance. No state reviewed classifies prepared bait as feed, but there are 50 states. This is a Year 2+ question when expansion happens.
  • Specific formaldehyde SDS and concentration for the inventory we're acquiring from the seller. Different concentrations change PPE requirements, ventilation sizing, and MAQ calculations. We need to pull the actual SDS sheets as part of the acquisition diligence.
Resolution Path

Items 1 and 2: licensed engineer, $1,500–$4,000. Item 3: one phone call, free. Item 4: defer to Year 2. Item 5: SDS pull during acquisition diligence, no cost.

All five uncertainties have clear, affordable resolution paths. None are deal-breakers.

The Four Regulatory Realities

  • Environmental compliance (EPA RCRA waste handling, 40 CFR 261.33, code U122) — procedural and manageable
  • Building code and occupancy classification — potentially the biggest cost variable, needs engineer early
  • Product regulation — almost certainly none (not food, not feed, not a marine fisheries product)
